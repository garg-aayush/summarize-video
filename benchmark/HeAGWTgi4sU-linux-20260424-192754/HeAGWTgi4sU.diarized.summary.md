# Rajkummar Rao and Ranveer Allahbadia on Learning English and Language Stigma

## TL;DR
Rajkummar Rao and Ranveer Allahbadia discuss their contrasting experiences with English language acquisition and the social pressures surrounding it in India. Rao learned English organically through listening and pattern recognition to navigate the Mumbai film industry, while Ranveer was forced into English in school, later requiring professional Hindi tuitions to regain the fluency needed for his infotainment content. Both speakers highlight the "colonial baggage" of English, where proficiency often dictates social standing and professional access.

## Key Points
- Rajkummar Rao learned English through organic immersion and "pattern recognition" by listening to others, rather than through formal classes (Rajkummar Rao).
- The necessity of English became apparent to Rao after the project *LSD*, as he realized Mumbai's professional environment and interview circuits operate primarily in English (Rajkummar Rao).
- Ranveer Allahbadia faced systemic pressure in school to speak English, noting that students were physically punished by teachers for speaking Hindi (Ranveer Allahbadia).
- Forced English fluency can degrade native language skills; Ranveer had to take professional Hindi tuitions to transition from conversational Hindi to the precise language required for on-camera infotainment (Ranveer Allahbadia).
- English proficiency in India is tied to "colonial baggage," creating a social dichotomy where non-speakers are looked down upon, while fluent speakers may be judged as acting superior (Rajkummar Rao).
- Acting skills, specifically the ability to mimic accents and listen intently, accelerated Rao's ability to pick up English sentence formations (Rajkummar Rao).

## Takeaways
- Language is acquired through environment and active listening rather than treating it as a school subject to be passed for marks.
- There is a significant difference between conversational fluency and the linguistic precision required for professional public speaking or "infotainment."
- The social stigma surrounding English in India persists as a remnant of colonialism, influencing how individuals are perceived in professional and social hierarchies.
- Leveraging existing skills (such as an actor's ear for accents) can significantly speed up the process of learning a new language.
